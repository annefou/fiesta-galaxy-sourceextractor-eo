## LinkedIn / blog version (~300 words)

**What if the tools that find galaxies could also help protect a wetland?**

We've gotten good at making research *reproducible* — Carpentries, CodeRefinery, FAIR4RS, Galaxy running real analyses in a browser. The next frontier isn't more compute; it's tools and metadata that **cross disciplinary boundaries**, so a method built for one science can be picked up, unchanged, in another.

A tool is only reusable across fields if its metadata travels with it: what input it expects, what it returns, how to run it. When that's in place, the disciplinary label stops mattering. So we tested the idea at its most extreme.

We took **Source Extractor** — the astronomy workhorse that detects stars and galaxies in sky images — and, without changing a line, ran it through **Galaxy** on **satellite night-lights** of Europe. It cataloged **453 lit settlements** in the Po Valley exactly as it catalogs stars: position, brightness, size. Cities as constellations.

Then we made it *useful*: overlaying that catalog on the **Natura 2000** network shows light pollution pressing on the **Po Delta**, a Ramsar wetland and migratory-bird refuge. It's still relatively dark (mean night radiance 0.96 vs 4.11 region-wide) — but **18.5% of the refuge is already lit, and 145 settlements sit within ~10 km.** Artificial light at night disrupts nocturnal birds, insects and bats, so this is a measurable biodiversity stressor, not just a pretty map.

A star-finder, measuring light pollution over a bird refuge — entirely in Galaxy. What tool from *your* field could cross over? (Companion to our BioImage.IO example: a microscopy model mapping lakes from space.)
